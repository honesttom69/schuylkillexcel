My wife thinks I'm nuts.

I spent a Saturday wiring up a $30 Fire TV Stick because my 6-year-old was watching a video about Minecraft builds and three autoplay clicks later he's watching something I can't even describe.

Every parent knows this. You hand your kid a screen because you need 20 minutes to cook dinner. YouTube's algorithm takes it from there. And where it goes is not where you'd send them.

## The Problem With YouTube Kids

The Shorts are the worst part. Rapid-fire clips designed to keep scrolling. My kid doesn't need that. No kid does. But you can't turn Shorts off. YouTube won't let you. It's how they keep engagement numbers up.

I looked at YouTube Kids. It's better but it's still algorithmic slop — AI-generated garbage content that technically passes the filter but exists only to farm views from children.

I needed something different. Not a parental control. Not a filter. A completely different way to give my kid YouTube without the parts designed to exploit him.

## What I Built

Sideloaded an open source YouTube app on a Fire TV Stick that does three things the real YouTube refuses to do:

1. **Completely removes Shorts.** Gone. Not hidden — gone.
2. **Blocks ads.** All of them.
3. **Blocks sponsored segments** inside videos.

My kid gets full-length videos. Minecraft builds. Star Wars lore. Nature documentaries. The stuff he actually wants to watch. Without an algorithm deciding what comes next.

Then I set up retro games on the same device. NES. Super Nintendo. Genesis. Nearly 9,000 games from the 80s and 90s. He plays the same ones I played when I was his age.

**Total cost: $30 and an afternoon.**

## The Bigger Point

My son asked me the other night if he could "watch the Minecraft guy" and I said sure without a single moment of anxiety.

That's new. That didn't used to happen.

I've been thinking about why this felt so satisfying to build. And I think it's because it's the opposite of what most tech companies want.

They want engagement. I want my kid to watch one thing, enjoy it, and then go outside.

They want autoplay. I want him to choose.

They want Shorts. I want him to have an attention span.

A $30 stick and some free software gave me more control over my kid's screen time than any $15/month parental control app ever did. Because the apps are all built on top of the same system that's causing the problem.

Sometimes the answer isn't a better filter. It's replacing the whole thing.
