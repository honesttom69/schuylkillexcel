I've been meaning to build a real website for years. Not "years" in the vague way people say it — I have actual dead WordPress installs and abandoned Namecheap setups to prove it. Every time I tried, I'd get far enough to realize the gap between what I wanted and what I could execute was wider than I thought. And I was never quite committed enough to hire someone, partly because I wanted to understand what was being built. I didn't want to just hand it off.

So the site sat in limbo. A half-finished thing I'd think about every few months, tinker with, and abandon again.

## What Changed

Nothing dramatic. There wasn't a single moment where I decided "today's the day." I've just been in a season of wanting to explore what's possible — thinking about career moves, what consulting could look like if I actually put myself out there, and how AI tools have changed the equation for someone like me.

I'd been using Claude Code for other projects and figured: why not point it at the website problem? Not as some magic button, but as a partner. Someone (something?) that could handle the parts I'd always gotten stuck on while I stayed in the driver's seat.

## My Web Dev Credentials (Such As They Are)

Let me be honest about my skill level: I'm a millennial who peaked at customizing his MySpace page. I know enough HTML to be dangerous, in the sense that I can get a website technically online but it'll look like it was built in 2006. The finer details of CSS, responsive design, deployment pipelines — that's where I'd always stall out and lose momentum.

I'm not a developer. I'm a consultant who needs a website. There's a difference.

## What We Actually Built

The stack ended up being Hugo (a static site generator) with a theme called hugo-profile, hosted on GitHub Pages. If that sentence means nothing to you, the important part is this: it's fast, it's free to host, and I actually understand how it works.

Here's what came together in a single working session:

- **Custom branding** — my brand colors, Gotham font, company logos, all wired into the theme properly
- **Service cards** for each consulting offering, laid out clean
- **My headshot** on the site (finally)
- **Custom CSS** so it doesn't look like every other template site
- **Domain migration** — schuylkillexcel.com was stuck on a suspended Bluehost account. We moved DNS to Cloudflare and pointed it at GitHub Pages. Total hosting cost going forward: zero dollars.

The whole thing cost me nothing beyond the domain I already owned.

## The Part That Surprised Me

I expected the broad strokes to go fine. Getting a site online isn't the hard part — it never was. It's the finer details that always killed me. The spacing that's slightly off. The font that won't load. The logo that looks crisp on desktop and blurry on mobile. That stuff is where I'd spend three hours, get frustrated, and close the laptop for six months.

This time, those details were the easy part. I'd say "the heading feels too close to the card below it" and it would just get fixed. Not in a "let me Google CSS padding for 45 minutes" way — in a "done, refresh the page" way. And when something wasn't quite right, I could describe what I wanted in plain English and iterate.

But here's the thing that actually mattered most: Claude also built me a guide. A plain-English SOP for how to write and publish new articles, update content, and maintain the site myself. That's the difference between "someone built me a website" and "I have a website I understand." I can change anything by hand now, and I have a reference that makes it straightforward instead of me reverse-engineering my own site six months from now.

## The Honest Take

This isn't an ad for AI. The site isn't perfect and I'll keep tweaking it. But the thing that's different this time is that it actually exists. It's live. It has my name on it and my services listed and it looks like something I'd be comfortable sending to a potential client.

I spent years stuck in a loop: want site, try to build site, hit wall, abandon site, repeat. What broke the loop wasn't some breakthrough in motivation. It was having a collaborator that could meet me where I was — uncomfortable with web dev but clear on what I wanted — and bridge the gap without taking the wheel away from me.

The site is at [schuylkillexcel.com](https://schuylkillexcel.com). I built it myself. I had help. Both of those things are true.
